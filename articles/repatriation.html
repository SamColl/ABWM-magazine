Relocation and Repatriation

On the 30th of January ABWM hosted an interesting and thoughtful discussion by Tricia Carter, a Cultural Intelligence Facilitator, on Global transitions; Relocation and Repatriation. The talk centered around the difficulties people face moving to a new place, especially one which is far from home and different in so many aspects to what one is used to and, conversely, when one moves back to their country of origin after having lived abroad for many years. In a wonderfully intimate and informal setting, members shared with Tricia some of their experiences with having to move to new places and also when they've gone back home for holidays. Cultural differences, language differences, simple things like weather and would it melt our candles were some of the topics that were touched on.



Relocating can be a momentous change which affects the whole family. Tricia touched on ways to ease that transition. The physical move, sometimes going more smoothly than the mental and emotional one. If it is a first time transfer to a different part of the world your reactions may be different to a new culture and environment than if you were a more seasoned mover. One's openness to such differences will also depend on one's individual personalities. For spouses, who may have had their own careers and financial independence and who now cannot work, a move can often lead to a loss of identity and a need to find their new role in the family dynamics.



Tricia spoke of 5 ways to well-being to help with such a transition.



  1. Be active. Do something! Join a local gym, a book club or indulge in a hobby you love.
  2. Take notice. Where we are in the moment.
  3. Connect. Our connection with people affects our mental well being.
  4. Keep learning. Take advantage of the concept of the university of the 3rd age.
  5. Give of your time. Even if it's volunteering in your child's school or a local NGO.


Children are also affected by the changes to their environment and can find the transition quite hard. Whether it is simply a weekly movie night at home for the whole family or playing board games. Taking out an atlas, helping your child locate your new destination was another suggestion. Searching up information together so they feel more prepared and discussing the different cultures and languages they may encounter would also help them understand the differences they may encounter.

Whilst relocation has been given much thought with companies offering relocation services to help with home rentals, schools and general knowledge of the country they are moving to, not much thought has been given to repatriation. Companies do not offer repatriation facilities to families who are returning home due to their overseas contract ending or most often simply because the working member of the family has retired and faces the reality of going back to what was once considered home. Repatriation is sometimes as difficult as Relocation. One of the challenges of going home is the cost of the growth you've experienced living in different countries and different cultures. After the challenges and changes of new places it's not always easy going home and settling down. Often, as some members pointed out in the course of the discussion, people back home haven't changed but you have. Leaving a place you have made friends, built a life full of memories can also be quite difficult. Some members attending the talk mentioned how they begin to distance themselves as a coping mechanism.

Tricia mentioned an interesting method to help with such transitions, called building a RAFT.

A RAFT of Reconciliation, Affirmation, Farewell and Think Destination.

Reconciliation - Where one lets go of unresolved issues and mends any bridges they feel they need to. It is only with proper closure of issues that one can move on without carrying mental baggage of unresolved problems.

Affirmation - of what you had in the place you have been staying and what it means to you. Acknowledging friends and their impact on you. Children are also affected by having to leave their friends, their schools, help them in acknowledging those relationships. Notes of appreciation go a long way.

Farewells - this is not just reserved for people. It can also mean to places which hold special memories. Farewell mementos help in the process of leaving.

Think Destination - this is where you and your family think of where you are going. For those going home, think of what changes have taken place within you and those that have, or have not, taken place at home. An interesting concept which allows you to move on in peace and face the challenges that are ahead of you.
